# Sequencing MEP Rough-In So Trades Don't Trip Over Each Other

You've got electrical, plumbing, and HVAC crews showing up the same week. The electrician runs conduit where the plumber wants to run 3/4-inch copper. The HVAC crew is supposed to install return ducts but the electrician is in the way. By day three, all three trades are standing around waiting, and you're bleeding $2k per day in overhead while nobody works.

This is a problem that money doesn't fix. What fixes it is sequence.

The standard sequence—electrical first, plumbing second, HVAC third—exists for a reason. But it only works if you plan it, communicate it, and enforce it. Most GCs don't, and jobs drag because trades are improvising.

## The Standard Order and Why It Exists

**Electrical goes first.** Electricians run the smallest infrastructure—wire and conduit. They don't need to know where plumbing or HVAC are going; they'll route around. They need open framing and access to the panel. Put them in first when the framing is done and the walls are open. They'll finish in 5-7 days on a typical residential job.

**Plumbing goes second.** Plumbers need to see where electrical runs (to avoid conflicts), and they need the same open walls and attic access. Their work is larger—3/4-inch and 1-inch runs—so they need to be strategic about routing. Plumbing takes 4-6 days on residential. They're installing main lines, rough-ins for fixtures, and venting.

**HVAC goes third.** HVAC crews install ductwork, the largest piece of the puzzle. They work around electrical and plumbing. Return ducts go up through walls and attic, supply ducts get sized based on the house layout. This takes 3-5 days. They're the last rough-in trade because they work with the largest pieces and need the most coordination space.

After HVAC comes drywall. Only then do you close the walls.

Why this order? Because each trade, when it goes second, needs to see what the first trade did. Electrical sees bare framing. Plumbing sees electrical and framing. HVAC sees electrical, plumbing, and framing. Reverse it, and someone's rerouting work.

This order also respects code. Electrical needs to be roughed and inspected before plumbing rough-in in most jurisdictions. Plumbing needs to be inspected before ductwork goes in (so inspectors can see the waste and vent lines). This isn't arbitrary—it's built into the permit.

## When the Standard Breaks

The standard sequence breaks in three situations: radiant floor systems, seismic regions, and complex commercial jobs.

**Radiant heat changes everything.** If you're installing radiant tubing in concrete or under the floor, that goes in before anything else—before electrical, before framing sometimes. The tubing has to be laid out, pressure-tested, and verified before the slab is poured or before the subfloor is finished. I've seen jobs where the electrical crew had to reroute their runs to avoid a radiant loop that should have gone in during framing. Plan radiant systems separately, with a detailed layout, and get it done before the other MEP trades show up.

**Seismic regions require coordinated bracing.** In California and the Pacific Northwest, mechanical contractors have to coordinate with structural. Ductwork requires seismic supports, and those supports sometimes share framing with bracing. The HVAC crew can't just run ducts without verifying that structural requirements are met. In these regions, have the structural and mechanical drawings cross-referenced during design. Don't find out mid-rough-in that HVAC ducts are in the path of seismic bracing.

**Complex commercial jobs need parallel trades.** On a 20,000-square-foot office build, you can't run electrical, plumbing, and HVAC strictly sequentially—the job would take four months. Instead, divide the building into zones. Run all MEP for Zone A first, drywall, then move to Zone B. This keeps trades moving and allows for overlap without conflict.

## Inspection Scheduling Tactics

Inspections create the hard stops in the sequence. You can't drywall until electrical is signed off. You can't close walls until plumbing is signed off. You can't finish until HVAC is signed off.

Schedule inspections in advance with the Building Department. Call at the start of framing and ask: "When can you inspect electrical rough-in? When can you do plumbing? When can you do HVAC?"

This tells you:

1. Whether the inspector schedules same-day (rare) or if you need to wait 3-5 business days
2. Whether inspections are bundled (one inspector for all MEP) or separate
3. Whether the inspector requires 48 hours' notice

With this information, you can tell the electrical crew: "You need to be done by Thursday so we can call inspection Friday and get signed off Monday." Electricians respect hard dates. They'll double up crews if needed to hit that date.

Have your sub-contractors do their own pre-inspections. Before calling the Building Department inspector, have the electrical contractor walk the rough-in with you. Look for missing junction boxes, undersized conduit, inadequate support. Catch these before the inspector does. A failed inspection costs three days (you fix, you wait for reinspection, you move on). Catching it in-house costs four hours.

## What to Do When One Trade is Late

It happens. Electrical crew gets pulled to another job. Plumbing runs late because they found a main water line that has to be replaced. HVAC can't get crews available for another week.

When one trade is late, stop and recalculate. Don't just wait.

If electrical is running late:
- Have plumbing rough-in prep work done. Run waste and vent lines in walls where electrical definitely won't go (exterior walls, far from the panel). This isn't ideal, but it keeps the job moving. Finish the rest of plumbing after electrical rough-in is done.
- Or bring in a second electrical crew to run the home run and finish faster.

If plumbing is running late:
- HVAC can start ductwork in the basement and crawlspace where plumbing isn't running. They can rough-in main return and supply lines while plumbing finishes the upper floors. Coordinate so they don't collide in the attic.

If HVAC is running late:
- Start drywall in areas where HVAC is done. If the first floor is roughed, drywall it while HVAC finishes the second floor.

The key: identify which areas are complete and move the job forward there. Don't hold the entire house because one trade is slow.

## Communication Before Anyone Swings a Hammer

A lot of MEP chaos comes from miscommunication. I've watched an electrician run conduit down a wall, a plumber wants to run copper there, and they argue about who goes where. This is a planning problem, not a on-site problem.

Before framing is done, sit down with the general contractor, electrician, plumber, and HVAC contractor. Review the rough-in plan:
- Mark electrical routes on the framing plan (conduit chases, panel location, main runs).
- Mark plumbing routes (main water line, waste and vent).
- Mark HVAC routes (main supply and return trunk lines).

This takes two hours and prevents a week of delays. Electrical adjusts their route if plumbing has a conflict. Plumbing moves if HVAC needs the space. Everyone sees the plan and knows what's expected.

Document it. Take photos of the framing with marked routes, or sketch a one-page plan. When the electrician says "I'm putting conduit there," you pull out the plan and show him the pre-agreed route.

## Three Things You Can Do Tomorrow

**Call your Building Department and get the inspection schedule for your next project.** Ask how many days between request and inspection, whether inspections are bundled or separate, and whether they charge per inspection. Write it down and use it to build your schedule.

**Create a one-page rough-in sequencing checklist.** Include: Electrical (framing done, panel accessible), Plumbing pre-meeting (rough-in plan reviewed, no conflicts marked), Electrical rough-in complete, Plumbing rough-in complete, HVAC ductwork installed, all inspections signed, drywall ready. Use it on every job. Consistency prevents surprises.

**On your next job with three or more trades, schedule a thirty-minute pre-rough-in meeting with all subs.** Print or sketch the rough-in plan on a sheet and review it together. Ask each contractor if they see conflicts. I've had conflicts surface in this meeting that would have caused three days of rework. Cost of the meeting: zero. Cost of rework: $3k-5k.
