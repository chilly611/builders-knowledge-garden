# Survival Guide for Supply Chain Spikes

Lumber was $450 per thousand board feet in 2020. It hit $1,600 in May 2021. It's back to $400 now. Copper wire went from $3 per pound to $5 in three months. Drywall disappeared for six weeks. Electrical equipment had a four-month lead time.

If you're bidding jobs and assuming stable material costs, you're gambling. Supply chain spikes hit every few years, and when they do, they eat margin or tank jobs. A contractor who understands volatility and can lock in pricing without taking inventory risk survives. One who doesn't, doesn't.

The key is simple: anticipate spikes, understand which materials are vulnerable, lock in pricing when you can, and develop vendor relationships so you're not caught flat-footed when supply is tight.

## Which Materials Are Most Volatile

Not all materials spike equally. Some are commodity-driven (lumber, steel, copper). Some are supply-chain driven (panels, appliances, electrical equipment). Some are stable (concrete, sand, labor).

**Lumber.** This is the most volatile. It's traded on the Chicago Mercantile Exchange. It moves based on housing starts, tariffs, shipping, and timber supply. A 30% swing in price over six months is normal. A $30k lumber budget can become $40k or stay at $28k depending on timing.

Monitor the Lumber Futures price (CME LS contract). It's public. When it starts trending up, you're three weeks ahead of the retail market. If you have jobs coming that need framing lumber, buy or lock in pricing before the spike hits. If the futures price is down, wait.

**Electrical components.** Wire, conduit, breakers, panels. These are manufactured goods with long lead times. A tariff or production disruption in Asia creates a shortage within weeks. During 2021-2022, some electrical items had 16-week lead times. A job you bid at 8-week lead time suddenly needs expedite fees or your timeline breaks.

Call your electrical supplier monthly and ask: "What's the lead time on [specific items]? Are there any supply concerns?" This takes five minutes and tells you whether to lock in pricing or wait. If lead times are creeping from 4 weeks to 8, prices are about to spike.

**Copper and steel.** Raw commodity metals. They track with global demand and geopolitical events. A trade war, shipping disruption, or surge in demand from Asia creates shortages. Copper especially—it's used everywhere (wire, plumbing, HVAC). During the post-2020 recovery, copper spiked and stayed elevated for 18 months.

Watch commodity prices online (Trading Economics, Kitco, Investing.com). You don't need to trade. You just need to see the trend. If copper is rising 2-3% per week, your plumbing supplier's prices are about to jump. Lock in if you can.

**Drywall and panels.** Gypsum and OSB are commodities. During the pandemic, drywall plants shut down and reopened slowly. For six months, you couldn't find drywall. Today, supply is fine, but the vulnerability is real. If housing demand spikes, drywall gets tight.

Ask your drywall supplier: "How's your inventory position? Any supply concerns?" Build trust. When supply gets tight, vendors prioritize repeat customers.

**Appliances.** Manufactured goods with global supply chains. During 2021-2022, kitchen appliances had four-month lead times. A homeowner who wants specific appliances needs them ordered three months before installation. If lead times lengthen, you either delay the job or buy what's available and eat the cost difference.

For appliance-heavy jobs, call the supplier at bid time and ask: "What's the lead time on [specific models]? Is there a price premium for expedite?" These details go in the bid.

## How to Lock in Pricing Without Taking Inventory Risk

Most GCs are small—you can't buy 10,000 board feet of lumber and store it. You'll run out of space and capital. But you can lock in price without taking inventory.

**Lumber:** Call your lumber supplier and ask if they offer price locks. Most do. You say, "I'm bidding a job that needs 8,000 BF of framing lumber. I want to lock in a price for 60 days. If I buy within 60 days, I get that price." The supplier charges a fee (typically 0.5-1% of the purchase price), but you've eliminated the price risk. You bid confidently, and if you win the job, you buy at the locked price.

For a $60k job with $8k in lumber, a 0.5% lock fee is $40. That's cheap insurance against a 10% lumber spike.

**Metals:** Copper, steel—call your supplier and ask about hedging or price agreements. Some suppliers will quote a price good for 30 days with a deposit. Others will put you in a commodity pool where you lock in a price range, not a fixed number.

I've worked with a plumber who locks in copper pricing every quarter. He calls his supplier on the first of the month and says, "What's your price on 1/2-inch and 3/4-inch copper tubing for jobs starting in the next 90 days?" The supplier quotes, he bids, and if he wins, he buys at that price. This removes the volatility from his estimate.

**Electrical:** Most electrical suppliers won't lock pricing, but they will quote a lead time. You bid assuming a certain lead time and price. If lead times extend beyond what you bid, you contact the homeowner and discuss. But you're not gambling—you know the constraints upfront.

**Appliances:** Order early and lock in. A lot of appliance suppliers will reserve equipment at a quote price for 30-60 days. When you're bidding a kitchen with high-end appliances, call the supplier and ask for a price good for 60 days. It costs nothing, and you have certainty for the bid.

## The Three Vendor Relationships Every GC Should Cultivate

Margins compress during supply spikes. What saves you is relationships.

**A primary lumber supplier.** This person should know your business. How many jobs you typically have going, what size jobs, what lead times you need. When supply tightens, suppliers prioritize repeat customers. I have a lumber supplier who, during the 2021 spike, called me and said, "Prices are about to jump. How much lumber do you want me to set aside?" Because I'd been buying from him for five years, I got allocation when other contractors couldn't. That saved me tens of thousands in margin.

How to build this: Use the same lumber yard for every job. Ask for a dedicated account manager. Pay invoices on time. In the off-season, call and chat. When supply gets tight, they remember you.

**An electrical supplier with local inventory.** You need someone who can source hard-to-find items quickly. During 2021, some electrical items had 16-week lead times nationally but your local supplier had stock. This person should know that you're in business, you pay on time, and you'll remember them when you're making choices.

How to build this: Spec your jobs through one or two electrical suppliers. Develop a relationship with the estimator or account manager. When you need to source something unusual, call them first.

**A concrete supplier with flexibility.** Concrete is usually straightforward, but weather delays or schedule changes can create problems. You need a concrete supplier who will work with you on short-notice pours, coordinate delivery timing, and give you a fair shake on price if you're a regular.

How to build this: Use the same concrete company for every job. Ask about their scheduling flexibility. When you're in a pinch and need an expedited pour, they remember that you've been loyal.

These relationships aren't transactional. You're not asking for discounts. You're asking for reliability and, when supply gets tight, for priority allocation. Suppliers give this to contractors who are worth their time.

## What to Do When a Material You Bid Doubles in Price

Sometimes you lock in price and the lock fails, or you bid before a spike and supply-chain chaos hits.

First: Check your contract. Does it have a material price escalation clause? Most small GC contracts don't, but you should add one. Something like: "If material prices exceed 10% of the bid amount, the contractor and homeowner will discuss adjustment." This gives you a conversation starting point.

Second: Contact the homeowner before the surprise hits. Don't wait until you order lumber and it costs 20% more. Call early: "I bid this job at lumber prices that were [price]. Lumber has moved to [new price]. This impacts the job cost by $[amount]. Here are our options: (1) I absorb the increase and adjust margin. (2) We adjust the price by [amount]. (3) We substitute with a less expensive material."

Most homeowners will negotiate if you're transparent. They'll reject you if you hit them with a change order after the fact.

Third: Look for substitutions. If framing lumber doubled, is there an alternative? Can you reduce lumber use or shift to engineered components? Can you value-engineer the job without changing the outcome? A contractor who can say "I can save you $2k by doing [this] instead" maintains the relationship.

Fourth: Don't take it out on your subs. If electrical components spiked and you underestimated, you eat some of it. You don't price-hike your subs. They're the people holding your business together.

## Three Things You Can Do Tomorrow

**Create a spreadsheet tracking material prices for the materials you use most.** Add columns for Date, Lumber Futures Price, Copper Price ($/lb), Electrical Lead Time (weeks), Drywall Price ($/SF). Update it monthly. Over six months, you'll see patterns. You'll know when to lock in and when to wait.

**Call three of your regular suppliers and ask about price-lock options.** "If I'm bidding a job that needs [specific material], can you offer me a price lock for 60 days? What's the fee?" Document their answers. Next time you bid a material-sensitive job, offer the homeowner a price-locked quote. It's a competitive advantage.

**Build one vendor relationship this quarter.** Pick the lumber yard, electrical supplier, or concrete company you use most. Call the owner or account manager. Tell them you're building your business and you want to work together long-term. Ask what they need from you as a contractor (on-time payment, advance notice, clear specifications). Commit to those things. Check in quarterly. This relationship will pay for itself during the next supply spike.
