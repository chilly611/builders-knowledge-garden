# Bidding Residential Remodels Without Leaving Margin on the Table

You walk into a 1970s ranch kitchen. The homeowner wants it gutted—new cabinets, countertops, flooring, walls opened up. You're thinking $45k. By the time you finish the walk, you realize you're looking at $67k. The difference? You didn't know what was hiding behind the walls.

Residential remodels kill contractors because they're not new construction. New construction has known conditions. Remodels have surprises. The job that should take three weeks takes four because of cast iron drains, aluminum wiring, or a load-bearing wall nobody mentioned. And when you bid $45k, you're already thin on margin—you have no room to absorb the shock.

The best protection is a site walk that's systematic. Not a stroll. A checklist.

## The Site Walk Checklist

Before you write a single number, you need to know three things: existing condition, hidden hazards, and required work the homeowner isn't thinking about.

Start outside. What's the roof condition? Residential remodel clients almost never budget for roof repair, but if you're pulling permits and the inspector notices missing shingles or rot, you own it. Look at the exterior walls—water damage, foundation cracks, settling. These don't cost money in a bid, but they tell you whether the house is structurally sound or if you're walking on eggshells.

Inside, bring a flashlight and get into the crawlspace or attic. This is where the real information lives. You need to see:

- **Framing**: Is it settled? Are there sistered joists or recent repairs? Settling tells you whether the house is stable or if your new walls and openings will cause cracks.
- **Electrical**: Is it knob-and-tube? Aluminum? Two-prong outlets everywhere? If the panel is original and the homeowner wants to upgrade kitchen circuits, that's a $3k-5k surprise they didn't budget for.
- **Plumbing**: Galvanized? Copper? Cast iron? If you're opening walls and find cast iron drains, assume you'll have to replace them. Cast iron in residential is $80-120 per linear foot plus removal and disposal.
- **HVAC**: If ductwork is in the attic, you'll see immediately whether it's insulated, if there are disconnects, if it's collapsed. Poor ductwork = you're inheriting a HVAC problem.
- **Insulation**: What's there, if anything? If you're moving walls or opening ceilings, you may have to upgrade insulation to code. That's labor and material.

Check under sinks. Look for evidence of leaks, rot, mold. Peek behind appliances if they're staying. The homeowner will tell you "nothing's wrong," but wet spot behind the dishwasher tells a different story.

## Three Line Items GCs Consistently Underprice

After walking hundreds of residential jobs, three categories eat margins worse than anything else:

**Waste removal and disposal.** Everyone underestimates this. A kitchen remodel generates 15-25 cubic yards of debris. A full bathroom remodel with wall demo, 8-12 yards. If you're carrying it off-site yourself, fine. If you're hiring a dumpster, it's $400-600 per pull. If you're assuming the homeowner will haul it, you're gambling. Most won't. I've seen jobs where waste removal was estimated at $1,200 and came in at $3,800 because the contractor had to make three dumpster pulls and pay for tip fees. Build in a 30% cushion and verify with the GC who owns the account where trash is going.

**Electrical work beyond scope.** The homeowner says "new lighting in the kitchen." You bid four can lights and three pendant fixtures. Then the electrician finds that the panel is at capacity, the service needs upgrading, and the existing circuits are undersized. That's suddenly $4k-6k you didn't bid. Before you pencil in electrical, assume that at least one circuit will need work beyond what the homeowner mentioned. Ask the electrician for a pre-bid walkthrough—$200-400 spent here saves $2k-4k later.

**Finish-grade adjustments.** This one's subtle. When you remove old flooring, you expose the subfloor. In a 1980s ranch, that subfloor might be uneven, wet, or settling. To get a level surface for new flooring, you may need to self-level, sister joists, or replace sections. This is labor-intensive and eats hours. In my experience, jobs that looked straightforward turned into $2k-3k in unexpected floor preparation because the subfloor was worse than expected. See it yourself before you bid.

## Markup Strategy by Job Size

Markup isn't negotiable based on client friendliness. It's based on risk and overhead.

For jobs under $15k (bathroom vanity, small kitchen refresh, basic repairs), your markup should be 35-40%. Why? The overhead cost—permits, site visits, scheduling, cleanup—doesn't scale down. A $5k job requires the same project management as a $25k job, so your percentage needs to be higher.

For jobs $15k-$50k, 28-32% markup is reasonable. This is the bread-and-butter zone where residential remodels live. You have enough scope to amortize overhead, but you're still dealing with unknowns. The margin needs to absorb surprises.

For jobs over $50k, you can operate at 22-28% if the project is well-scoped. At this size, you're usually doing more extensive work with clearer existing conditions. The client is also more likely to accept change orders if something comes up.

But here's the thing: these markups assume you know what you're pricing. If you're guessing, add 5 percentage points.

## When to Walk Away

The worst margin is negative. Sometimes a job looks good until you start digging, and then it's a black hole.

Walk away if:

- The homeowner is financing through a HELOC or cash-out refi and watching every penny. These clients micromanage, request scope creep, and fight change orders. Your margin disappears into phone calls and meetings.
- The existing condition is worse than the scope implies and the homeowner says "just fix it if you find it." You can't price risk you can't quantify. Require a change order process or decline.
- The homeowner has done significant work themselves already. DIY electrical, DIY plumbing—these create liability and code issues. You'll spend half your time fixing corners they cut.
- The home is old (pre-1950) and the owner hasn't had a professional inspection. Older homes have settlement, hidden structural damage, and pest damage that surfaces mid-job. Either require a pre-work inspection or bid high enough to absorb the unknown.

## Three Things You Can Do Tomorrow

**Schedule a thirty-minute walkthrough with your most trusted electrician.** Bring a scope of work for a kitchen remodel you're bidding. Have him walk the panel, the circuits, the attic routing, and give you a feel for electrical surprises. Buy him coffee. This relationship pays for itself on the second job.

**Create a one-page residential remodel walkthrough checklist.** Include: roof condition, exterior water damage, attic/crawlspace findings, electrical type and capacity, plumbing material, existing HVAC condition, subfloor condition, foundation cracks, settled framing. Use it on every bid. Consistency catches surprises.

**On your next three bids, force yourself to add a line item for "Existing Condition Contingency."** Make it 8-10% of the hard costs. Don't hide it in markup. When you're bidding at $52k and the homeowner sees a $4,200 contingency line, it signals that you've thought about risk. Most will accept it. Those who won't are exactly the clients you should decline.
