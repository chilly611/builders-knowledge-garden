# Managing Cash Flow on 90-Day Jobs Without Killing Your Bank Account

A 90-day remodel job is the sweet spot—big enough to pay attention to, short enough that you can see the finish line. But cash flow on a 90-day job is treacherous. You're burning through materials and labor in the first 30 days while the first payment doesn't arrive until day 35. You're two payrolls deep, materials are on the credit card, and the homeowner is going through her second kitchen without cabinets.

I've watched GCs with solid businesses run out of cash on $60k jobs because they didn't think about draw timing. A 90-day job requires structure: draw schedule, retainage planning, deposit timing, and a process for chasing late payments without burning the relationship.

## The Draw Schedule That Actually Works

A draw schedule is a roadmap for payment. It ties payment to progress, not to "whenever we feel like asking." You build the schedule before work starts.

A standard draw schedule for a 90-day job has four or five draws:

**Draw 1 (10%):** On signing. This is your deposit. Typical is 10-20% depending on the scope and homeowner risk. I use 15% as default. On a $60k job, that's $9,000. It covers your deposit to suppliers, insurance, and initial materials.

**Draw 2 (30%):** At 30% completion. For a 90-day job, that's day 27. You've framed, rough-ins are done, inspections are scheduled. You've spent heavily on labor and materials. The 30% draw—$18,000 on a $60k job—covers your first two payrolls and material purchases.

**Draw 3 (30%):** At 60% completion. Day 54. You're in the thick of finish-out—drywall, mechanical finish, trim. This draw is $18,000. It gets you through the second third of the job when you're burning the most cash on wages and materials.

**Draw 4 (20%):** At 90% completion. Day 81. Flooring, paint, fixtures, final trim. You're near the end. This draw is $12,000. It carries you through final labor and finish materials.

**Draw 5 (10%):** On completion and walkthrough. This is your final payment. $6,000. You don't walk off until you have it.

Why this structure? Because the work doesn't follow payment. You need cash for drywall, tape, and mud in week 2. You don't get paid for it until week 5. The draw schedule closes that gap.

But here's the thing: this assumes the homeowner pays on time. They won't always.

## Retainage Planning: Keeping 10% as Leverage

Retainage is the amount you hold back until the very end. It's leverage.

I hold 10% retainage on every job over $15k. That means my four draws are structured differently:

**Draw 1 (12%):** $7,200 (10% deposit, 2% held back)
**Draw 2 (27%):** $16,200 (30% less 10% hold)
**Draw 3 (27%):** $16,200 (30% less 10% hold)
**Draw 4 (18%):** $10,800 (20% less 10% hold)
**Final (16%):** $9,600 (10% payment plus 10% retainage release)

Retainage matters when there's a punch-list item the homeowner doesn't want to pay for. You've finished the job. There's a cabinet door that doesn't close right, or touch-up paint needed. The homeowner says "I'm withholding $2,000." If you don't have retainage in your contract, you're fighting to get it. If you do, you say, "I'll fix that. Here's the punch list. Once it's done, you get your final payment." It's not a threat. It's clarity.

On a $60k job with 10% retainage, you're holding $6,000. That's enough to cover a week of punch-list work and keep the homeowner honest about paying for agreed-upon work.

## When to Take a Deposit and How Big

Deposits are your insurance policy. A bigger deposit means less of your own cash in the job.

For jobs under $25k, take 20%. The homeowner is less likely to back out, and 20% is enough to cover mobilization, permits, initial materials, and two weeks of labor.

For jobs $25k-$75k, take 15%. This is the sweet spot. It's substantial enough to matter without feeling exploitative. On a $60k job, $9,000 covers your initial spend and shows the homeowner is serious.

For jobs over $75k, 10% is acceptable. These are big enough that the homeowner has usually refinanced or saved, and they're thinking longer-term. A smaller deposit is a competitive advantage.

But here's the rule I live by: **never start work without the deposit in the bank.** Not "the homeowner promised," not "they're getting it Friday." Don't pour a foundation until you see the deposit clear. Don't order cabinets until you have cash. I've had jobs where the homeowner's financing fell through on day 3. If I hadn't held the deposit, I would have been out $5k in materials already.

Get the deposit in writing as a signed agreement. Have your accountant set it aside as a liability (money held for a specific job). Spend it only on that job's actual costs. If the job cancels, you refund it minus any costs you've incurred and work done.

## Chasing Late Payments Without Burning the Relationship

Every GC faces a homeowner who says "the check is in the mail" on day 40. You need that draw. You have a payroll Friday.

First move: prevention. Make payment terms explicit in your contract. Not "payment within 30 days." Say "Payment due upon invoice, prior to next draw." Make it clear that invoicing happens at specific milestones and payment is expected before you mobilize for the next phase.

If a payment is late:
- **Day 5 overdue:** Call the homeowner. Friendly. "Hey, I have my payroll coming up. Did you get my invoice?" Nine out of ten times, they forgot or it's stuck with their accountant. A phone call fixes it.
- **Day 10 overdue:** Email with the invoice and a copy of the contract terms. Not angry. Professional. "Per our contract, payment was due on [date]. Please let me know if you need a copy of the invoice or if there's an issue with the amount. I need to close this out by [date 3 days out] to stay on schedule."
- **Day 15 overdue:** Call again. Tougher tone. "I've had to float the last draw and my payroll is due Friday. I need a timeline for payment. If I don't have it by [date], I'll have to pause work until we get caught up."

That last call is the line. You're not threatening. You're being clear about the boundary. If the homeowner knows that nonpayment results in a work pause, they'll prioritize.

If work does pause: pause it fully. Don't have electricians on-site while plumbing waits. It costs money and looks soft. A full pause sends a message.

Most homeowners will pay by day 15 when they realize you're serious. I've had two situations in fifteen years where I actually paused work over payment. Both jobs got caught up within a week, and I never had another payment issue with those clients.

Document all communication. Email is your friend. If you have a conversation, send a follow-up email: "Per our conversation today, I understand the check will be ready Thursday." This creates a paper trail.

## One More Thing: Subcontractor Payment

Your subcontractors are the real cash-flow problem. If you've collected 30% from the homeowner on day 27, you owe your electrician, plumber, and framing crew by day 30. You're short by 10 days.

Here's how I handle it:

I don't hold retainage from subs. I pay them within 7 days of invoice. In exchange, I ask them to invoice in phases that match my draw schedule. When I get a draw, I pay the corresponding sub invoice immediately. This builds loyalty. Subs know they'll be paid on time, so they prioritize my jobs.

For new subs, I run a credit check and verify references. I don't extend net-30 to contractors I don't know. I pay deposit-upon-invoice for the first two jobs, then we can talk about net-10 or net-20.

This costs me a bit of cash float, but it's cheaper than having a sub place a lien or bail on your job mid-rough-in.

## Three Things You Can Do Tomorrow

**Write out your draw schedule for your next 90-day job.** Use the four-draw or five-draw model. Tie each draw to a milestone (framing, rough-ins, drywall, finish, complete). Share it with the homeowner before signing the contract. Ask her to acknowledge it. This isn't a negotiation—it's clarity.

**Create a spreadsheet showing cash in and cash out for a typical 90-day $60k job.** Put the draw dates on the x-axis and payroll, material costs, and sub payments on the y-axis. Show when you're negative (spending your own cash). This will show you exactly how big a gap exists and why the deposit and draw schedule matter.

**On your next job, set a reminder for day 5, day 10, and day 15 after each draw due date.** On day 5, call the homeowner if you haven't received payment. On day 10, email the contract and invoice. On day 15, have the pausing-work conversation. This isn't harsh. It's professional. The homeowner will respect the clarity, and you'll get paid.
